H.O.N.

Review Board Protocol

12 reviewer disciplines · 7 review areas · Scoring rubric · Copy-paste LLM prompt · Interactive scorecard · Last updated: 2026-05-29
Section 1 — How to Use This Document
This document defines the H.O.N. review board process. Each review assigns one reviewer (human or LLM) to one of the 12 disciplines below and asks them to evaluate the app across all 7 review areas from that discipline's specific lens. To run a review, copy the prompt from Section 5, replace [DISCIPLINE] and [DISCIPLINE_LENS] with the chosen role, and provide the reviewer with access to the codebase (or a screenshot tour of the app). Record scores in the Section 6 scorecard. For a comprehensive pre-ship review, aim to run at least 4–6 disciplines. For a quick sanity check, the three highest-priority disciplines are: Product Manager (overall product sense), iOS Engineer (technical correctness), and Certified Strength Coach (domain accuracy).
Section 2 — The 12 Reviewer Disciplines
01
Product Manager
Core Loop Clarity
Does the app solve a real, felt problem? Is the core habit loop (log → get insight → come back) immediately obvious? Can a user articulate the value proposition in 30 seconds? Is feature prioritization coherent — nothing feels bolted on? Are the 3 most important screens earning their place?
02
UX Designer
Interaction & Hierarchy
Information architecture across 6 tabs. Visual hierarchy on each primary screen. Interaction patterns (taps, swipes, long presses) are consistent and discoverable. Delight moments are earned, not forced. The design system is applied uniformly. Nothing requires a tutorial to use.
03
iOS Engineer
Technical Correctness
SwiftUI best practices: @Observable store usage, view re-render efficiency, proper use of environment. Performance: no main-thread blocking, background task safety, memory management. Architecture coherence: single store pattern, cache invalidation, startup gate (isLoaded). No data races in refreshAnalytics().
04
Data Scientist
Analytics Integrity
Statistical soundness of the readiness score (0–100 range, confidence intervals, input signal weighting). e1RM formula selection (Epley vs. Mayhew — are the reps boundaries appropriate?). Strength decay model: is 0.7%/day after 14 days evidence-based? Do the caches represent the data faithfully? Are insights actionable or merely decorative?
05
Certified Strength & Conditioning Coach
Exercise Science Accuracy
Progression logic: is the weight suggestion aligned with progressive overload principles? Recovery recommendations: does the readiness engine reflect real recovery science? Are compound/isolation exercise classifications correct? Does the trainer tab produce programs that a real CSCS would endorse? Are there any contraindications for special populations (rehabilitation, elderly)?
06
Sports Psychologist
Motivation Architecture
Feedback loop quality: does post-set coaching reinforce identity (I am someone who trains) vs. only performance (you hit your numbers)? Streak mechanics: do they create positive tension or unhealthy anxiety? Comeback messaging: does it reduce shame for the lapsed user? Is the "H.O.N. philosophy" (Habit Over Numbers) embodied in the actual UX, or just the name?
07
Behavioral Economist
Habit Formation Design
Commitment devices: does completing onboarding feel like a commitment the user wants to keep? Loss aversion: how does the app leverage (or avoid over-leveraging) streak loss fear? Variable rewards: is there enough surprise in coaching narrative to drive re-engagement? Friction analysis: is there appropriate friction before data deletion and zero friction before logging?
08
Accessibility Expert
Inclusive Design
WCAG 2.1 AA compliance: contrast ratios, focus management, keyboard navigation (external keyboard). VoiceOver completeness and accuracy of announcements. Dynamic Type across all screens at Accessibility Extra Large. Minimum 44pt tap targets universally. Color independence: no information conveyed by color alone. One-handed usability.
09
Security & Privacy
Data Protection
HealthKit data handling: minimal scope requested, proper purpose strings, denied-permission graceful degradation. Local storage: is sensitive health data encrypted at rest? No PII logged to console or telemetry. Network traffic: zero outbound data without explicit consent. Permission scope: only data types actually needed are requested. Data deletion is complete and verified.
10
Growth & Marketing
Virality & Acquisition
Shareable moments: what's screenshot-worthy? (PRs, streak milestones, coaching narrative). Referral potential: what would make a user say "you have to try this app"? Onboarding funnel: where do new users drop off and why? App Store presence: does the current product support a compelling preview video and screenshots? Category positioning vs. MyFitnessPal, Strong, Hevy.
11
QA Engineer
Defect Coverage
Edge cases: zero data, extreme values, same-day multiple sessions, mid-session kill, concurrent updates. Error states: what happens when any operation fails? State machine completeness: can any user action leave the app in an undefined state? Regression risk: which changes to SeedStore are most likely to introduce regressions? Input validation coverage.
12
Retention Specialist
Long-Term Value
D1/D7/D30/D90 retention prediction: which features drive which retention windows? Churn signals: what does the data look like for a user about to churn — can they be saved? Re-engagement mechanics: is the comeback experience compelling enough for ghost users? Long-term value progression: does the app get more valuable the more you use it, or plateau early? Identity investment over time.
Section 3 — The 7 Review Areas
1
First Impression & Onboarding
0–60s: What does a brand-new user see immediately? Is the value proposition clear before any interaction? Does the visual design create confidence?
0–5min: Onboarding flow completeness, cognitive load of each step, time to reach first interactive home screen.
0–24h: Post-onboarding prompt to action — does the home screen immediately make the user want to log their first workout? Does the first-time trainer tab experience feel worth returning for?
2
Core Workout Loop
The fundamental unit of value: starting a session → adding exercises → logging sets → completing. Is this loop frictionless? Does it feel fast during an actual workout? Is every step intuitive without prior knowledge? Does the coaching narrative add real value after each set, or is it noise? Does the post-session feel selector feel meaningful or like a chore?
3
Intelligence Layer
Readiness score: is it credible and actionable? Does it actually change behavior? Weight suggestions: are they calibrated correctly? Coaching narrative: does it feel like a knowledgeable coach or a random motivational generator? Trainer tab plans: do they improve demonstrably with more data? Does the app feel smarter after 4 weeks than after 1 week?
4
Progress & Analytics
Data visualization quality: are charts readable at a glance? Insight actionability: does the user know what to do after viewing the Progress tab? StrengthLab depth: is the composite strength score meaningful? Is the strength decay model honest and useful? Does the analytics section reward long-term users with richer insight than newcomers?
5
Retention Mechanics
Streak design: is it motivating without being anxiety-inducing? Celebration quality: are milestone moments proportionate and genuine? Comeback flows: does the app make returning after a gap feel welcoming, not shameful? Identity reinforcement: over time, does the user start to see themselves as "a person who trains"? What brings them back on day 22 vs. day 1?
6
Technical Quality
Stability: zero crashes in normal use. Performance: tab switch <100ms, launch <2s, analytics <500ms background. Data integrity: no data loss, no corrupted cache, all calculations verified accurate. Architecture coherence: single store, proper threading, debounced analytics. Error handling: graceful degradation on any failure.
7
Ship Readiness Verdict
Overall score from the reviewer's discipline lens (1–5). Top 3 strengths with specific references. Top 3 blockers with specific references. Ship / Conditional Ship / No-Ship recommendation. One-paragraph executive summary that the CEO could read and immediately understand the state of the product.
Section 4 — Scoring Rubric (1–5 per area)
1
Broken
Feature is absent, non-functional, or actively harmful. Blocks core use case. Ship blocker.
2
Below Par
Present but significantly below expectations. Creates friction, confusion, or erodes trust. Needs work before ship.
3
Baseline
Meets minimum acceptable standard. Does the job without inspiring confidence. Shippable if not a core differentiator.
4
Exceeds
Notably good. A user would positively remark on this area. Creates differentiation from competitors.
5
Best-in-Class
Sets a new standard. Would be referenced as a model example in the category. Memorable and remarkable.
Section 5 — The Review Prompt

Replace [DISCIPLINE] and [DISCIPLINE_LENS] for each reviewer. All other content is fixed.

# H.O.N. App Review — [DISCIPLINE] Perspective ## Context You are reviewing H.O.N. (Habit Over Numbers), a SwiftUI iOS fitness app built with a single @Observable store (SeedStore). The app's core philosophy is that consistent habit formation matters more than optimizing numbers — the name itself is a manifesto against obsessive metric-chasing. ## App Architecture Summary - Six tabs: Home (readiness dashboard), Trainer (AI workout plans), Workout (active logging), Progress (history + charts), StrengthLab (e1RM analytics + decay model), Settings - Single store: SeedStore (@Observable), loaded once on startup behind an isLoaded gate - Three performance caches: lastPerformanceCache (O(1) per-exercise lookup), exerciseHistoryCache (20-session cap), homeCache (pre-computed readiness, trends, hints) - Analytics pipeline: refreshAnalytics() runs on background thread with UUID token debounce; commits to main only if token still matches - Coaching narrative: WorkoutNarrativeEngine assembles phrases from phrase_bank.json using a pick() rotation (UserDefaults-backed, prevents repeats) - e1RM formula routing: Epley (2–10 reps), Mayhew (11–15 reps), exact (1 rep), 0/excluded (>15 reps) - Readiness engine: ReadinessEngine.compute(log:) produces score 0–100, confidence, delta, coachingNote - Strength decay: peakE1RM × retentionFactor — no decay for 14 days, then −0.7%/day, floor at 50% ## Key Source Files - iOSApp/Models/Models.swift — all data models (Exercise, SetRecord, WorkoutLogEntry, etc.) - iOSApp/App/ContentView.swift — tab structure, ContentView - iOSApp/Engines/ — ReadinessEngine, StrengthAnalyticsEngine, WorkoutNarrativeEngine, etc. - iOSApp/Views/ — screen views grouped by tab area - iOSApp/Services/ — HealthKit, persistence layer ## Your Role You are reviewing this app as a [DISCIPLINE]. Your lens is: [DISCIPLINE_LENS] ## Review Instructions Evaluate the app across these 7 areas. For each area, provide: - A score from 1–5 using the rubric below - 2–4 specific findings (what works, what doesn't, what's missing) - At least one file reference where applicable (e.g., "WorkoutNarrativeEngine.swift line ~X") - The most important single improvement for this area from your discipline's perspective Area 1: First Impression & Onboarding (0–60s, 0–5min, 0–24h) Area 2: Core Workout Loop (logging, feedback, completion) Area 3: Intelligence Layer (readiness, coaching narrative, recommendations) Area 4: Progress & Analytics (data visualization, insight quality, actionability) Area 5: Retention Mechanics (streaks, celebrations, comeback flows, identity) Area 6: Technical Quality (stability, performance, data integrity) Area 7: Ship Readiness Verdict (overall rating, top 3 strengths, top 3 blockers, ship/no-ship) ## Scoring Rubric 1 = Broken or absent (ship blocker) 2 = Present but significantly below expectations (needs work before ship) 3 = Meets baseline expectations (shippable) 4 = Exceeds expectations (notable quality, creates differentiation) 5 = Best-in-class, sets a new standard (remarkable, memorable) ## Tone Requirements - Be direct and specific. Vague observations ("the UX could be improved") are not useful. - Reference specific screens, features, or code patterns when making claims. - Do not soften findings to protect feelings. If something is broken, say it's broken. - Do not inflate scores to seem balanced. A 3 means acceptable, not great. - Prioritize your top 3 findings by impact on ship decision. ## Output Format For each area: Score: X/5 Strengths: [bullet list] Issues: [bullet list] Key improvement: [one sentence] At the end, write a one-paragraph Executive Summary that a non-technical CEO could read in 60 seconds to understand: - Where the app is genuinely strong - The 1–2 things that must be fixed before ship - Your overall confidence level in the product - Your ship recommendation: SHIP / CONDITIONAL SHIP (fix X first) / NO-SHIP
