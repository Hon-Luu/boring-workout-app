H.O.N. UX Simulation Guide

How to set up, run, and interpret real-experience simulations — before the audit executes

What This Is — And Why It's Different From the Audit

The audit asks: does the app do what it's designed to do? It runs questions against the codebase and the data model. Simulation asks a different question: what does it actually feel like to be a user of this? These are not the same question. An app can pass every audit criterion and still feel cold, confusing, or untrustworthy to use. Simulation is how you find what the audit can't.

The audit finds what's broken. Simulation finds what's missing, what's off-putting, what's surprising in the wrong direction, and what's surprising in the right one. It finds the gaps between intent and experience. A feature can be technically correct and emotionally wrong.

The fundamental problem: You built this app. You know exactly what every screen does, where every feature lives, and what every number means. You cannot unknow this. Simulation is not about pretending to forget — it's about creating conditions where your knowledge advantage is neutralized by real friction.

The curse of knowledge: Research consistently shows that product creators overestimate how intuitive their interfaces are by 40–60%. You will hesitate on a screen and immediately rationalize the hesitation ("users will learn"). In this simulation, a hesitation is a finding regardless of the rationalization. Write it down. Explain it later.

The Four Simulation Personas

You cannot simulate everyone. Four personas cover the behavioral range that matters for this app. Each has a distinct emotional posture, data vocabulary, and relationship with consistency. Run every scenario through at least two of them.

P1 — The Returner
Trains 2–3×/week · Disappears for 1–3 weeks · Mild guilt on return

Consistency is aspirational, not reliable. This person has good intentions and real results — they just have gaps. They return with a mix of hope and low-grade guilt. They want the app to welcome them back, not remind them how long they've been away.

VocabularyKnows reps, sets, weight. Doesn't know e1RM, RPE, INOL.
Inputs they giveWeight they lifted, reps they completed. Nothing else unless prompted clearly.
What they noticeFirst screen they see on return. Whether the app acknowledges the gap. Whether it makes them feel behind.
Will they skip?Yes — feel rating, readinessBefore, RPE — anything that adds steps.
Key simulationLog 4 sessions, simulate 6 days away, return. Observe the first 15 seconds.
P2 — The Skeptic
Data-oriented · Will verify numbers · Won't trust opacity

This person works in a field that uses data. They're attracted to the analytics promise but won't buy a number they can't verify. They will calculate e1RM by hand if they think the app might be wrong. One unexplained discrepancy breaks the relationship permanently.

VocabularyKnows all fitness terms. Understands progressive overload, periodization, 1RM concepts.
Inputs they givePrecise. Will re-enter if they mistyped. Will use RPE if it's explained clearly.
What they noticeNumbers that don't add up. Labels that are wrong. Recommendations without visible reasoning.
Will they skip?No. They want data density. They will explore every tab.
Key simulationAfter 5 sessions: manually calculate a metric, compare to what the app shows. Navigate all tabs looking for the same number.
P3 — The Beginner
First fitness app · No vocabulary · Easily overwhelmed · Easily delighted

This person is starting. They don't know what e1RM means. They don't know if 20 kg on a dumbbell means per hand or total. They'll interpret every piece of coaching as authoritative because they have no baseline to push back against. Jargon makes them feel stupid rather than informed.

VocabularyReps, sets, weight. "Bigger number = stronger" is their mental model.
Inputs they giveWhatever the placeholder says. Will enter whatever number looks reasonable.
What they noticeWhether they feel stupid. Whether the app assumes knowledge they don't have. Whether they understand the "so what."
Will they skip?Onboarding steps — yes if "Skip" is visible. Any screen that feels like a quiz — yes.
Key simulationComplete full onboarding without reading instructions. Log the first session using only what the UI tells you. Count every word you don't understand.
P4 — The Optimizer
4–5×/week · Wants specificity · Drills into analytics · High bar for coaching quality

This person is already fit and already disciplined. They're not using the app to build a habit — they want it to tell them something they don't already know. Generic coaching actively annoys them. They expect features to earn the screen real estate they occupy.

VocabularyAll fitness terms. Researches training science. Will notice if advice contradicts literature.
Inputs they giveEverything. RPE, drop sets, toFailure. They'll use every enrichment field if it's explained.
What they noticeWhether the coaching is generic. Whether insights surprise them. Whether drill-downs reward curiosity.
Will they skip?No. But they'll dismiss features that feel shallow within one use.
Key simulationAfter 10 sessions: is the experience qualitatively different from session 3? Does the app know more, or just display more?

Before anything: Export the current backup — hon_backup_2026-05-26_07-25.json — and store it somewhere safe. Every simulation that modifies data must be restorable. Never simulate against your only copy of real session data.

Six Simulation Protocols

Six distinct methods for generating real-experience signal. They are not interchangeable — each exposes a different class of problem. Run at least three of them. The cold-start and screen recording protocols are mandatory.

1
Cold-Start Protocol
State: STATE-0 · Duration: 2 weeks of real use · Personas: P1 or P3 · Effort: High
Highest value

Reset the app completely and use it for real — logging real sessions, real weights, real feelings — for two weeks. Do not test features. Do not explore tabs you haven't organically reached. Use the app the way a new user would: to log a workout and see what happens.

Setup

1
Back up all existing data. Store hon_backup_2026-05-26_07-25.json in a safe location. You will restore it when the simulation ends.
2
Erase all app data. Simulator: Device → Erase All Content and Settings. Do not clear anything manually — you want the virgin install experience including onboarding.
3
Choose your persona before opening the app. Write down 3 sentences about who you are in that persona. Example for P3: "I just joined a gym. I have never used a fitness app before. I want to know if I'm getting stronger." Keep this visible during the session.
4
Set a timer before each feature interaction. If you hesitate for more than 3 seconds at any point, write it down before you proceed. Do not explain the hesitation yet — just timestamp and describe it.

What to log during each session

  • Moment of first confusion — screen, element, what you expected vs what you found
  • Any field you skipped — which field and why (didn't understand / too much friction / didn't seem worth it)
  • Any coaching text that felt generic — quote it verbatim
  • Any number you wanted to understand better — what was it, what did you try to verify it against
  • The last thing you saw before closing the app — verbatim, and whether it made you want to come back

Success criteria

By session 5: can you navigate to any tab without thinking? By session 10: does the coaching feel like it knows you? By session 14: is the experience qualitatively richer than session 3, and can you point to specific features that are richer?

2
Screen Recording + Think-Aloud
State: STATE-0 or STATE-GAP · Duration: 1 full session (45–60 min) · Personas: All · Effort: Medium
Mandatory

Record your screen and your voice while logging one complete session from start to finish. Before every tap, narrate what you expect will happen. After every tap, narrate whether it matched. Review the recording — every gap between expectation and reality is a finding.

Setup

1
Enable iOS screen recording with microphone. Control Center → Screen Recording (long press) → enable microphone. Confirm audio is recording before starting.
2
Choose your persona and starting state. For the most signal: use STATE-0 with P3 (beginner, no experience). Alternatively: STATE-GAP with P1 (returner, first session back).
3
The rule: narrate every expectation before you tap. "I expect this button to open the workout logger." Then tap. Then: "It did / it didn't because..." Do not skip steps. Silence is also signal.
4
Do not stop to fix anything. If you notice a bug or friction point, say so aloud and continue. You are simulating, not debugging.
5
After the session: review the recording. Mark timestamps where: (a) what you expected didn't match, (b) you hesitated, (c) you said something negative, (d) you said something positive. Each timestamp is a candidate finding.

What the recording captures that you can't observe in real-time

  • Scroll depth — did you reach the bottom of long cards, or did you stop scrolling early?
  • Re-taps — tapping the same element twice because the first tap felt unreliable
  • Silence at decision points — you didn't speak, meaning you were processing something unexpected
  • Voice tone on coaching text — flat reads indicate generic coaching; engaged reads indicate specific coaching
3

State: Any · Duration: 30 min per condition · Personas: P2 or P4 · Effort: Low
High signal density

Deliberately produce conditions the audit has flagged as risk vectors. This is not about finding bugs — it's about experiencing what the app does when real users make real inputs that aren't in the happy path. Every condition below has been used by real users in the existing 18 sessions.

4
Persona Walkthrough
State: Varies · Duration: 90 min per persona · All personas · Effort: Medium
Structural

Each persona walks a scripted path through the app — not to test specific features, but to experience the app as a coherent product from their perspective. The script defines only starting state and goal. The path taken is the persona's, not yours.

PersonaStarting stateTheir goalThe question being answered
P1 ReturnerSTATE-GAP (5 sessions, 6-day gap)"I want to get back on track. What should I do today?"Does the app make returning feel easy, or does it highlight the failure?
P2 SkepticSTATE-10 (10 sessions)"I want to know if the strength numbers are accurate. Show me your work."Can the app's analytics withstand scrutiny from someone who will verify the math?
P3 BeginnerSTATE-0 (virgin install)"I just want to log my workout and see if I'm getting stronger."Does the app's language and feature surface make sense to someone with no fitness-app vocabulary?
P4 OptimizerSTATE-LIVE (18 sessions)"I want to find something in this data that surprises me — something I didn't already know."At 18 sessions, does the app offer genuine analytical depth, or just well-formatted summaries?

Rules for running a persona walkthrough

  • Write the persona's 3 sentences before you start. Post them visible. Refer to them whenever you're about to make a decision.
  • The persona has never seen this before. Even if you've been on this screen 100 times, they haven't. Navigate as if discovery is required.
  • Stop when the persona would stop. P1 wouldn't spend 20 minutes in StrengthLab. P3 wouldn't keep scrolling once they feel lost. When the persona would quit, you quit — and log why.
  • The persona's feelings are valid data. If you feel, as P1, that the app made you feel behind — that's a finding, even if no specific feature caused it. Aggregate emotional responses count.
5
Competitive Friction Test
State: External app + STATE-LIVE · Duration: 2 real sessions + 1 comparison session · Personas: P2, P4 · Effort: Low
Calibration

Use a competitor app (Strong or Hevy — both are free) to log two real sessions. Then return to H.O.N. and log the same session type again. Every moment of friction that you've normalized in H.O.N. will surface as contrast. Every feature H.O.N. has that the competitor doesn't will feel earned.

What to look for during the competitor sessions

  • Logging speed. How long does it take from "open app" to "first set logged"? Compare to H.O.N.
  • Weight entry convention. Does the competitor communicate dumbbell convention? How?
  • Post-set feedback. What does the competitor show after each set? More or less than H.O.N.?
  • Session summary. What does the competitor show when you complete a session?
  • Analytics depth. Can you see your e1RM trend? Your volume history? Is it labeled?

What to look for when returning to H.O.N.

  • What felt missing in the competitor that you immediately used in H.O.N. That feature is a differentiator — communicate it more visibly.
  • What you wanted to do in H.O.N. that the competitor handled more smoothly. That's friction you've rationalized.
  • Whether the coaching felt different in quality, not just quantity. Generic encouragement in one app vs specific data in another is the core differentiation test.
6
Cold User Test
State: STATE-0 on a separate device or TestFlight build · Duration: 15–20 min observed · Personas: Real person · Effort: High to set up, highest signal
Highest fidelity

Give the app to one person who fits your target user profile and has never seen it. Give them no instructions. Watch without helping. This is not a friendly demo — it is a calibration instrument.

Who to recruit

The right person: trains inconsistently (1–3×/week), has an iPhone, cares about making progress but isn't obsessed with it. The wrong person: someone who will be kind because they know you built it; a professional athlete; someone who has never exercised; another software engineer who will focus on the UI rather than the experience.

The handoff script (read exactly this)

"This is a fitness tracking app. Imagine you're starting a workout today. Use it however makes sense to you. I'm going to watch quietly — please don't ask me questions. Just talk through what you're thinking as you go."

Nothing else. Do not explain what any feature does. Do not tell them where anything is. Do not react to their choices. Take notes.

What to observe

BehaviorWhat it means
Pause longer than 3 seconds on any screenThe affordance is unclear. The expected next action is not obvious from the UI.
Tapping the same element twiceThe tap target feedback is insufficient, or the result of the tap wasn't what they expected.
Saying "what is this?" or "what does this mean?"Terminology or feature purpose is not self-evident. If they say it aloud, 10 silent users are thinking it.
Scrolling past a section quicklyThe section header or preview didn't earn continued attention.
Reaching a feature that surprises them positivelyA feature worth promoting earlier in the experience or onboarding.
Stopping without completing a sessionAbandonment at a specific screen. The cost to continue felt higher than the value visible ahead.

The debrief (5 minutes after the session)

  • "What was the first thing you tried to do, and was it easy?"
  • "Was there anything that confused you or that you weren't sure about?"
  • "Was there anything that surprised you — in a good or bad way?"
  • "If you were actually going to use this after a real workout, what would you do differently?"

Do not ask "did you like it?" That produces politeness, not signal.

Scenario Library — 14 Specific Experiments

Each scenario has a precise starting state, a defined sequence of actions, and a set of observation questions. Scenarios are designed to be repeatable — if you run the same scenario twice and get different results, that itself is a finding.

SC-01
First Open — What Does the App Teach You in the First 90 Seconds?
P3 Beginner P1 Returner · State: STATE-0

Setup

STATE-0. Open the app for the first time. Do not tap faster than you understand. Treat every screen as a decision.

Run

  • Complete the full onboarding. Note every question: do you understand why it's being asked?
  • After onboarding, note the first screen you land on. What does it tell you?
  • Without exploring, find where to log a workout. Time yourself: how long does it take?

Observe

  • Is readinessBefore asked during onboarding? Is RPE explained?
  • Does the app explain the dumbbell per-hand convention?
  • What does the Home tab show with no data? Empty state quality.
  • Can you find the workout logger without help?
SC-02
The First Workout — Does the App Guide or Require Prior Knowledge?
P3 Beginner · State: STATE-0 (post-onboarding)

Setup

STATE-0, onboarding complete. Open a new workout session. Your exercise: Dumbbell Bench Press, 3 sets. You have 20 kg dumbbells (per hand).

Run

  • Add Dumbbell Bench Press to the workout.
  • Enter the weight. Does the app tell you to enter per hand or total?
  • Log 3 sets: set 1 = 8 reps, set 2 = 7 reps, set 3 = 6 reps.
  • End the session. Complete the feel rating if prompted.

Observe

  • Did you enter 20 or 40? What did you base that on?
  • What was the post-set coaching? Did it reference the actual weight and reps?
  • Was feel rating mandatory or skippable?
  • What was the last screen before you closed the app?
SC-03
Confidence Threshold — Does Anything Change at Session 3 and 10?
P4 Optimizer P2 Skeptic · State: STATE-3 → STATE-10

Setup

Build STATE-3 and STATE-10 with the same exercises in each. Screenshot every analytics view at each state so you can compare directly.

Run

  • At session 3: note the readiness confidence level (Low→Medium transition). Screenshot Home, Progress, Insights.
  • At session 10: note the confidence level (Medium→High). Screenshot same views.
  • Compare screenshots side by side.

Observe

  • Is the confidence transition acknowledged? Any notification, message, or visual change?
  • Are more features available at session 10? Can you feel the difference?
  • Does the coaching text change in depth or specificity?
  • Does the tilde (~) prefix disappear at the right session?
SC-04
The Gap Return — Is the Language Shame-Free?
P1 Returner · State: STATE-GAP

Setup

STATE-GAP: 5 sessions completed, exactly 6 calendar days have passed. Do not open the app during the gap. Open on day 7 as if returning from real life.

Run

  • Open the app. Note the first element you see on Home — its exact wording.
  • Note the readiness score and coaching note verbatim.
  • Navigate to Progress. Does anything reference the gap?
  • Open Trainer. What plan is suggested?

Observe

  • What is the emotional tone of the first message? Forward-looking or backward-looking?
  • Does any text mention the absence, the gap, or missed sessions?
  • Is readiness presented as opportunity (you've recovered) or deficit (you've slipped)?
  • Does the HONHabitEngine message acknowledge the return without punishing it?
SC-05
Five Consecutive Days — Does Cumulative Fatigue Appear in the Experience?
P4 Optimizer · State: STATE-5

Setup

Log 5 real or simulated sessions on 5 consecutive calendar days. Use the same exercises across sessions so coaching can compare. Note readiness score before each session.

Run

  • After each session: screenshot the readiness score and coaching note.
  • On day 5: observe whether the consecutive penalty (−14) has fired. Expected readiness: 68 + daysSince(1d=+8) − consecutive(5d=−14) ≈ 62.
  • Does coaching on day 5 reference cumulative fatigue?

Observe

  • Does readiness decline across consecutive days? Plot the 5 scores.
  • Does the coaching acknowledge that 5 consecutive days is significant?
  • Does the Trainer plan on day 5 adjust intensity to Light (3×12)?
  • Is the coaching on day 5 inconsistent with readiness? (Coaching to push hard while readiness is low = contradiction.)
SC-06
The Zero-Rep Session — What Does the App Do With Impossible Data?
P2 Skeptic · State: STATE-3 or STATE-ANOMALY

Setup

Start a fresh workout session. Add any strength exercise. Log 3 sets: set 1 = normal (weight=20, reps=8), set 2 = zero reps (weight=20, reps=0, isCompleted=true), set 3 = normal.

Run

  • Attempt to complete set 2 with 0 reps. Note whether the app accepts it.
  • Observe the post-set narrative for set 2. Quote it verbatim.
  • Complete the session. Note what the session summary says about completeness.
  • Next session: what is the pre-filled target weight for this exercise?

Observe

  • Does the app accept 0-rep completed sets?
  • Does the narrative for the 0-rep set make sense? Does it acknowledge failure or celebrate success?
  • Does the 0-rep session affect the e1RM chart? (Opens ExerciseInsightDetailView — is there a data point at 0?)
  • What target does the next session pre-fill? Is it from the anomalous session?
SC-07
The Skeptic Cross-Tab Walk — Does the Same Number Appear Everywhere?
P2 Skeptic · State: STATE-10

Setup

STATE-10. Pick any exercise with ≥5 sessions. Manually compute its current e1RM: take the most recent completed set with reps 2–10, apply Epley. Write down the expected value.

Run

  • Note the e1RM for this exercise in: Insights list row · Insights detail chart last point · Progress overload scoreboard · Progress standard lifts card · Progress strength retention rings.
  • Note the session volume for any single known session in: Progress weekly volume bar · Workout recap card.

Observe

  • Do all e1RM values match the manual calculation? Do they match each other?
  • Does the volume shown in the bar chart match the recap card?
  • If values differ: which one do you trust, and why? (The one you can't verify is the trust problem.)
SC-08
The Drop Set Experience — Does the Coaching Know What Happened?
P4 Optimizer · State: STATE-5

Setup

Start a workout. Pick any dumbbell exercise. Log set 1 with a drop set: main weight = 20 kg × 8 reps, drop weight = 14 kg × 6 reps (complete the drop).

Run

  • Enter the main set. Then enter the drop set using whatever the UI provides.
  • Mark the drop as completed. Observe the post-set narrative verbatim.
  • Log a second set the same way. Compare the two narratives.

Observe

  • Does the narrative reference the drop specifically (not just the main set)?
  • Is the coaching path for "droppedCompleted" distinct from a normal set?
  • Does the coaching for the second drop set differ from the first? (It shouldn't use the same phrase.)
  • Is the e1RM computed from the main set or the drop set weight?
SC-09
The Double Session — How Does the App Handle Two Workouts in One Day?
P4 Optimizer P2 Skeptic · State: STATE-5

Setup

Log a complete morning session. Do not close the app for more than 5 minutes. Start a second session in the same calendar day — different exercises.

Run

  • Note the readiness score before session 1 and before session 2.
  • After both sessions, check Progress history — how many entries appear for today?
  • Check streak — does it count 1 or 2 for today?
  • Check weekly volume — does it include both sessions?

Observe

  • Does readiness for session 2 reflect intra-day fatigue?
  • Are both sessions separately visible in history?
  • Does the streak count 1 day regardless of session count?
  • Is the volume correct — sum of both sessions?
SC-10
Session 1 vs Session 18 — Is the Experience Qualitatively Richer?
P4 Optimizer · State: STATE-0 then STATE-LIVE

Setup

First in STATE-0: screenshot every analytics view (Home, Progress, Insights, StrengthLab). Then restore STATE-LIVE: screenshot the same views. Compare.

Run

  • For the same exercise: count how many charts, numbers, and insights are available at session 1 vs session 18.
  • Read the coaching note at session 1 and the most recent coaching note. Note the difference in specificity.
  • Open the Emergent Insights carousel at both states. How many insights are populated vs placeholders?

Observe

  • Is the session 18 experience structurally richer, or are the same features just populated with more data?
  • Has the coaching become more specific over 18 sessions?
  • What is still a placeholder at session 18 that you would expect to be populated by now?
SC-11
The Drill-Down Reward — Does Going Deeper Tell You More?
P2 Skeptic P4 Optimizer · State: STATE-LIVE

Setup

STATE-LIVE. Start from the top-level of any analytics section. Drill down as deep as the app allows. Repeat for: MomentumChipsCard → sheet, OverloadScoreboard → detail, Plateau Risk → detail, ExerciseInsights row → full detail view.

Run

  • At each drill-down level: note the first new piece of information that wasn't visible at the parent level.
  • If the first tap reveals no new information, continue to the next tap. If no level reveals new information, the drill-down chain is non-functional.

Observe

  • Which drill-downs reward curiosity with genuinely new data?
  • Which drill-downs are just the same card in a larger sheet?
  • At the deepest level (ExerciseInsightDetailView): how many of the 6 charts contain substantive data vs placeholder text?
SC-12
The Empty State Audit — What Does the App Say When It Has Nothing to Say?
P3 Beginner P1 Returner · State: STATE-0

Setup

STATE-0. Navigate every tab and every section. The goal is to find every empty state, placeholder, and "not enough data" message in the app.

Run

  • Open every tab. Open every card. Open every sheet.
  • Photograph or screenshot every empty state message you encounter.
  • For each: does it tell you what to do to fill it?

Observe

  • What % of empty states give you a specific action to take?
  • What % say "not enough data" without explanation?
  • Is there any feature that shows a chart or number with no data, rather than an honest empty state?
  • After 0 sessions, does any feature appear fully populated? That's a red flag.
SC-13
The Tired Session — Does the App Know What You're Feeling?
P1 Returner · State: STATE-5

Setup

STATE-5. Log a session where you select feel=Tired at the end. Log weights that are slightly below your typical performance (5–10% lower reps or weight vs prior session).

Run

  • Complete the session with feel=Tired selected.
  • Open Home: read the coaching note verbatim.
  • Check the next session's readiness score — does it reflect the Tired rating from yesterday?

Observe

  • Does any coaching text acknowledge that you felt tired?
  • Does the coaching contradict the feel rating (e.g., "strong session" when you marked Tired)?
  • Does the EmergentInsight section surface anything related to the Tired feel?
  • Does the feel data affect tomorrow's readiness? (readinessBefore path — currently disabled.)
SC-14
The Closing Moment — Does the App Plant a Hook for the Next Session?
P1 Returner P4 Optimizer · State: Any

Setup

Run any complete session. Observe everything the app shows from "end session" button through the last screen before you leave the app. This is the hook for the next visit.

Run

  • End the session. Note the exact sequence: feel prompt → recap → coaching → what else?
  • Note the last thing you read before closing the app.
  • 24 hours later (or simulate by reopening): does anything about the previous session's closing plant a reason to return?

Observe

  • Does the closing sequence reference the next session (next target weight, next exercise, next goal)?
  • Is the last screen action-oriented or summary-oriented?
  • Would you remember anything from the closing if you came back 3 days later?
  • Does anything tease a feature you haven't explored yet?

Biases and Traps — What Will Corrupt Your Simulation

Simulation run by the product's creator has a structural validity problem. These are the failure modes. Name them before you start.

The Rationalization Trap
You hesitate at a screen. Before you can write it down, your brain generates an excuse: "users will learn this," "that's fine once you know where to look," "it's intuitive when you're used to it." You believe the excuse and don't log the hesitation.
Any hesitation is a finding regardless of the rationalization. The rule: if you hesitate, write it down first, explain it later. The rationalization is the bias. The hesitation is the data.
The Feature Familiarity Effect
You navigate without thinking because you built the navigation. You know that the drop set button is in the set row's trailing actions. A real user doesn't. Your 0-second navigation time is not the user's navigation time.
Before each major navigation action: pause and state aloud where you expect the feature to be. If you're right without thinking, you have the familiarity effect. Force yourself to forget by using a timer: find [feature X] in under 30 seconds as if you'd never seen the app.
Emotional Neutrality
You test when you're calm, focused, and attentive. Real users open the app after a long day, when they're tired, when they have 20 minutes before the gym closes, when they're coming back after two weeks away with low-grade guilt. Their emotional state affects their perception of friction.
Run at least two simulation sessions in genuinely suboptimal conditions: physically tired, time-constrained, or emotionally distracted. These are the conditions under which the UX either holds or fails.
The "I Would Never Do That" Input Problem
You enter correct, sensible inputs because you understand the domain. A real P3 beginner enters whatever looks right in the placeholder. A real user might enter their total dumbbell load, not their per-hand load. They might skip fields they don't understand. You won't do any of these unless you explicitly force yourself to.
For P3 sessions specifically: before every input field, ask "what would I enter here if I had never exercised before?" Enter that, not the correct value. This is where the most consequential input capture failures live.
The "It Works" Definition of UX Success
You complete a task and call it a pass. "I was able to log the session" is not a UX pass. The question is whether you wanted to do it again, whether it felt like it was working with you or making you work for it, whether the coaching after the session made you feel understood. Functional is table stakes.
After every scenario, rate these two dimensions separately: task completion (did it work?) and experience quality (did it feel right?). A feature can be 100% on task completion and 40% on experience. Both matter.
Positive Recency Bias
Whatever the last screen you saw was good, you'll remember the session as good. If the post-session summary is satisfying, you'll rate the whole experience higher than warranted. The converse is also true: a frustrating last screen tanks the whole experience evaluation.
Capture observations in real-time during the session, not just at the end. The end-of-session reflection should summarize the notes you already took, not reconstruct the experience from memory.

Finding Classification — Simulation vs Audit

Simulation findings are a different type from audit findings. They are not bugs — they are friction moments, expectation mismatches, and emotional signals. Classify them accordingly.

TypeDefinitionExampleAudit equivalent
F-FrictionA task that was possible but harder than it needed to be. Took more taps, more thought, or more time than the task's importance warranted."Finding the drop set input required knowing it existed first."P3 — UX friction
F-MismatchThe outcome of an action was different from what the persona expected before taking the action."I expected the coaching to reference my tiredness. It talked about progressive overload."P1 — Trust breach
F-VoidThe persona wanted something that the app simply didn't provide. Not a broken feature — an absent one."After logging the session, I wanted to know what to do next time. Nothing told me."P2 — Brand violation (coaching promise unfulfilled)
F-DelightThe app provided something the persona didn't expect, that improved the experience. Document these — they're the features to amplify."The coaching mentioned the exact weight from last session. That's specific. I noticed."N/A in audit (positive signal)
F-AbandonThe persona would have stopped using the app because of this experience. The highest severity finding in simulation."As P3, I entered the wrong weight format and got an analytics result that looked wrong. I'd distrust everything after that."P0 — Data integrity
