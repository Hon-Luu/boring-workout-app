H.O.N

Health OS — every feature, every formula, every citation

1. What is H.O.N?

H.O.N (Health OS) is a strength and cardio tracking application for iOS that goes beyond simple rep-and-set logging. Every metric the app produces is derived from peer-reviewed exercise science. The goal is to tell you not just what you lifted, but how efficiently you trained, how close you are to your genetic potential, and whether your body is ready to train hard today.

Design Philosophy

Most fitness apps record numbers. H.O.N interprets them. The three guiding principles are:

  • Evidence over heuristics. Every formula cites its source. No "proprietary algorithms."
  • Cross-modal awareness. Strength training and cardio are treated as correlated dimensions, not separate silos. A hard HIIT week affects your strength readiness score. A deload week in the gym affects your cardio fatigue interpretation.
  • Progressive depth. The app starts simple — log a set, see a trend. As more data accumulates, it surfaces deeper signals: INOL zones, fatigue-adjusted e1RM, energy system synergy, and training efficiency quartiles.

Initial Setup

Body Weight (required for tier classification)

Settings → Strength Profile → Body Weight (kg). Body weight is used in three separate calculations:

  1. Relative strength ratios (e1RM ÷ body weight)
  2. PSI normalization (fiber load ÷ BW⁰·⁶⁷)
  3. Bodyweight exercise effective load (pull-ups, push-ups: body weight is the resistance)
Important: Without body weight, relative-strength tiers default to "Intermediate," PSI normalization is disabled, and tier classifications are unavailable.

HealthKit data does not yet feed the Readiness score numerically, but is displayed alongside it for context. The app also writes completed strength and cardio workouts back to Apple Health.

2. Logging a Strength Workout

Starting a session

Tap "Start Workout" on the Home screen or from the Workout tab. A session timer begins. You can add exercises in any order. The app pre-fills weights and reps from your most recent session as targets, shown in grey. Log actual values in the white fields.

Sets, Weight Entry & Equipment

The weight you enter depends on equipment type:

EquipmentEnterEffective Weight Used in Calculations

EZ BarTotal loaded weightMax(entered, 10 kg bar)
Straight BarTotal loaded weightMax(entered, 6 kg bar)
DumbbellPer-hand weightentered × 2 (bilateral total)

Bodyweight—Your logged body weight
Assisted Pull-Up / DipCounterbalance weightBody weight − counterbalance

This ensures that "Barbell Bench Press 60 kg" and "Dumbbell Bench Press 30 kg/hand" both report comparable bilateral loads for analytics purposes.

Drop Sets

After completing a main set, tap the drop-set icon. Enter the reduced weight and the reps performed. The app keeps both the main set and the drop set in all calculations.

Why drop sets don't distort your e1RM: A heavy main set (e.g. 100 kg × 3) followed by a drop set (80 kg × 8) can produce different e1RM estimates: Epley gives 110 kg for the main set, and 101 kg for the drop. The app takes the maximum of both, preventing drop-set training from creating false regressions in your trend line. This is called the drop-adjusted e1RM.
Validation: If the drop weight equals or exceeds the main set weight, the app shows a warning. A "drop set" by definition uses a lighter load.

RPE & Feel Ratings

Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE, 6–10)

An optional per-set field on a 6–10 scale where 10 = true 1RM (zero reps left), 9 = 1 rep in reserve, 8 = 2 in reserve, etc. When entered, the app computes an RPE-adjusted e1RM that estimates what you could have done fully rested.

RPE-Adjusted e1RM (Zourdos et al., 2016)
RIR = 10 − RPE
totalReps = actualReps + RIR
e1RM_adj = weight / (1 − totalReps / 40.9)
Source: Zourdos MC et al. (2016). Novel Resistance Training–Specific RPE Scale Measuring Repetitions in Reserve. J Strength Cond Res. 30(1):267–275.

Session Feel Rating (Tired / Normal / Strong)

Logged once per session at finish. Maps to a cost multiplier that scales the session's fatigue load:

RatingCost MultiplierInterpretation
Tired0.85×High perceived effort for load achieved — possible overreaching or poor sleep
Normal1.00×Neutral baseline
Strong1.15×Low perceived effort — body is fresh, underloaded, or super-compensating

Consecutive feel streaks (2+ sessions of the same rating) trigger coaching notes. Three strong sessions in a row suggests it is time to add load. Three tired sessions in a row suggests deload or prioritise sleep.

3. Estimated One-Rep Max (e1RM)

The e1RM is the foundation of every strength metric in H.O.N. It estimates the maximum weight you could lift for exactly one repetition, derived from any working set you perform.

Why estimate rather than test? Testing a true 1RM is fatiguing, risky, and impractical weekly. Estimation from submaximal sets (2–10 reps) is validated at ±2–5% accuracy and allows trend tracking across every session without disrupting training.

Formula Selection by Rep Range

H.O.N uses two peer-reviewed formulas, selected by rep range, because no single formula is accurate across all rep ranges:

Epley Formula — ≤ 10 reps (Epley, 1985)
e1RM = weight × (1 + reps / 30)
Best accuracy at 2–10 reps. Error ≈ 2–5%. Simple linear model validated in multiple free-weight populations. Source: Epley B (1985). Poundage Chart. Boyd Epley Workout.
Mayhew Formula — 11–20 reps (Mayhew et al., 1992)
e1RM = weight / (0.522 + 0.419 × e−0.055 × reps)
Exponential decay model. Outperforms Epley at higher rep ranges (11–20). Validated at ±3–6% for 10–20RM sets. Source: Mayhew JL et al. (1992). Muscular endurance repetitions to predict bench press strength in men of different training levels. J Sports Med Phys Fitness. 32(1):37–42.
1-Rep Set
e1RM = weight (exact — no estimation needed)
> 20 reps
e1RM = 0 (excluded)
Formula error grows to 15–30%+ at very high rep counts. Sets beyond 20 reps are excluded to prevent contaminating trend data with unreliable estimates.

Bilateral Deficit Correction (Dumbbell Only)

When both arms work simultaneously (dumbbell exercises), the bilateral force produced is approximately 8% less than the sum of each arm's independent maximum. This is the bilateral deficit.

Bilateral Adjustment (Botton et al., 2016)
bilateralE1RM = (perHandWeight × 2) × Epley_or_Mayhew × 0.92
Applied only to dumbbell equipment. Source: Botton CE et al. (2016). Neuromuscular Adaptations to Unilateral vs. Bilateral Strength Training in Untrained Young Males. Eur J Sport Sci.

How e1RM Drives All Other Metrics

The e1RM from every session is the input to:

  • Session trend points — plotted on the e1RM trend chart (raw + rolling average)
  • PR step chart — a point is added only when a new all-time maximum e1RM is achieved
  • Relative strength ratio — e1RM ÷ body weight, classified into tiers
  • INOL — uses e1RM as the reference 1RM to compute intensity %
  • PSI (fiber load) — intensity relative to rolling best e1RM, raised to the 1.8 power
  • Composite Strength Score — via relative strength tiers

4. Tonnage (Volume)

Tonnage (also called "training volume" or "volume load") is the total mechanical work done in a session or exercise block.

Tonnage per Set
tonnage = effectiveWeight × reps + dropWeight × dropReps
For dumbbells, effectiveWeight = perHandWeight × 2. Includes drop set contribution.

Tonnage is displayed in the Exercise Insights view as "Tonnage per Session (kg·reps)" and for dumbbell exercises as "Tonnage per Session (kg·reps, per hand)". The per-hand form is used because dumbbell loads are typically logged per hand.

Scientific basis: Volume load (sets × reps × weight) is one of the strongest predictors of hypertrophic adaptation and strength gain. Meta-analyses by Krieger (2010) and Schoenfeld et al. (2017) showed dose-response relationships between weekly volume and muscle growth up to ~10–20 sets per muscle group per week. Tracking tonnage trend identifies accumulation phases and deload timing.

Weekly Volume per Pattern

In the Category Analytics section (Progress → swipe to Categories), the app shows average weekly tonnage per movement pattern over the last 6 weeks. This reveals imbalances — e.g. a lifter doing 12 sets of chest per week but only 6 sets of back per week will see horizontal push tonnage far outpacing horizontal pull.

5. INOL — Intensity-Normalized Overload Load

INOL (sometimes called Intensity-Normalized Overload Load) is a training load metric that accounts for both the intensity (weight relative to 1RM) and volume (total reps) of a session. It is derived from Prilepin's Table, the original Soviet periodization framework for Olympic weightlifters, adapted for general strength training by Hristo Hristov.

INOL per Set
intensity% = (effectiveWeight / referenceE1RM) × 100
INOLset = reps / (100 − intensity%)
INOL_session = Σ INOLset across all sets (including drop sets)
referenceE1RM = all-time best e1RM for that exercise. Intensity% is capped at 97.5% to prevent division by zero on true max attempts. Drop set contribution is added separately.

INOL Zones

INOL ValueZoneInterpretation
< 0.4LowInsufficient stimulus — too few reps, too light, or both. Unlikely to drive adaptation.
0.4 – 0.8ModerateLight-to-moderate work. Good for deload or technique focus sessions.
0.8 – 1.5OptimalProductive training zone for most lifters. Balances stimulus and recovery.
1.5 – 2.0HeavyIntense training. Appropriate for experienced lifters in accumulation phases.
> 2.0OverreachingExcessive load. Sustainable for 1–2 weeks before planned deload required.

Experience-Calibrated INOL Targets

The optimal INOL centre shifts with training experience, because beginners fatigue faster and recover more quickly from lighter loads while advanced lifters need more total work to drive adaptation:

6. Rep Decay

Rep decay measures how quickly your rep output drops across consecutive sets of the same exercise in a single session. It is computed as the ordinary least-squares slope of (set index → reps completed) for the most recent session.

Rep Decay (OLS Slope)
slope = OLS((0, reps_set1), (1, reps_set2), …, (n-1, reps_setN))
units: reps per set (negative = declining, positive = ascending)
Requires ≥ 2 completed sets with reps > 0 in the last session.

Interpreting Rep Decay

SlopeScoreInterpretation
−0.5 to −1.5100Optimal — controlled fatigue accumulation. Sets are challenging but not collapsing.
−1.5 to −2.570Moderately steep — fatiguing fast. Consider 30s more rest or 1 fewer set.
−0.5 to 0.065Too consistent — sets may be too easy, or rest periods too long.
0.0 and above40Ascending reps — warm-up sets not stripped, or load is trivially light.
≤ −2.530Extreme drop-off — severe fatigue. Reduce total volume or weight immediately.
Scientific basis: The set-to-set rep decline pattern under constant load was characterised by Willardson (2005, J Strength Cond Res) and Ratamess et al. (2007). A decline of 0.5–2.0 reps/set at 70–80% 1RM with 2-minute rest is consistent with productive hypertrophy training. Steeper declines indicate PCr depletion or insufficient rest (Schoenfeld BJ, 2016, Science and Development of Muscle Hypertrophy).

7. Session Efficiency Score

Session efficiency answers the question: how much strength improvement did you get per unit of fatigue invested?

Session Cost (fatigue-weighted)
cost_per_set = reps × (e1RM_set / refE1RM)^1.8 × min(e^(α × setIndex), 1.35)
session_cost = Σ cost_per_set × feelMultiplier
where α = fatigueDecay (0.03–0.10 by tier), feelMultiplier ∈ {0.85, 1.0, 1.15}
The (e1RM/ref)^1.8 term mirrors the force–velocity relationship: heavier sets cost disproportionately more per rep. The exponential term models intra-session fatigue accumulation across sets. Drop sets add their own cost (without the set-index multiplier). Feel rating scales the total.
Efficiency Score (per session)
efficiency = Δrolling_avg_e1RM / session_cost
Δrolling_avg = this session's 5-session rolling avg − previous session's rolling avg. Positive = net gain relative to cost.

Efficiency Labels

Efficiency is ranked against your own historical distribution for that exercise (requires ≥ 4 sessions):

Q1–Q3AverageNormal return for you on this exercise
< Q1 (bottom 25%)Below avgLow return — volume may be excessive or recovery insufficient

Scientific basis: The exponential intra-session fatigue model is derived from phosphocreatine resynthesis kinetics (Greenhaff et al., 1994, Am J Physiol). The (intensity)^1.8 exponent reflects the nonlinear relationship between relative load and neuromuscular fatigue (Schoenfeld, 2016). Feel multipliers based on session RPE research (Foster et al., 2001, J Strength Cond Res).

8. Fatigue-Adjusted e1RM

Your raw e1RM from set 3 is lower than what you could have done set 1, because fatigue accumulated. The fatigue-adjusted e1RM recovers the rested capacity implied by performing that output under accumulated intra-session fatigue.

Fatigue-Adjusted e1RM (per set)
adj_e1RM_i = rawE1RM_i × min(e^(α × i), 1.35)
where i = set index (0-based), α = fatigueDecay by tier
session_adj_e1RM = max(adj_e1RM_i) across all sets in the session
Cap of 1.35× prevents extreme adjustments on very late sets where noise dominates. The best (highest) fatigue-adjusted e1RM in the session is used as the session point.

The fatigue-adjusted trend is shown as a separate line in the e1RM chart and is averaged 50/50 with the raw e1RM trend in the Composite Strength Score's Level pillar and Momentum pillar. This prevents the score from always showing an optimistic picture while still crediting training capacity that is partially hidden by intra-session fatigue.

9. PSI — Physiological Stress Index (Fiber Load)

PSI is the app's most sophisticated strength measure. Rather than simply totalling tonnage, it estimates the actual mechanical stress placed on muscle fibers, weighted by each muscle group's physiological cross-sectional area (PCSA) and its activation level during the exercise.

Session Fiber Load (Raw PSI)
For each set of each exercise:
relIntensity = min(setWeight / refE1RM, 1.0)
activationWeight = Σ (pctMVC_muscle × PCSA_muscle)
setContribution = relIntensity^1.8 × reps × activationWeight
rawPSI = Σ setContribution across all exercises in session
refE1RM = rolling best e1RM for that exercise (never retroactively recalculated). pctMVC = EMG-derived % of Maximum Voluntary Contraction. PCSA in cm² normalised to 70 kg reference body. The 1.8 exponent reflects the nonlinear fiber recruitment–force relationship.
Normalized PSI Variants
normalizedPSI = rawPSI / bodyWeight^0.67
leanPSI = rawPSI / leanMass^0.67
musclePSI = rawPSI / muscleMass^0.67
The 0.67 (⅔) power is the allometric scaling exponent for body mass and muscle force capacity, established by Nevill et al. (1992, J Sports Sci) and applied in cross-sectional strength research to allow fair comparison across body sizes.

PSI Trend

The app computes OLS slope of raw PSI over the last 6 weeks, expressed as %/week. This answers: Is the total mechanical stress on your muscles growing, stable, or declining? A flat or declining PSI trend despite consistent training often indicates a plateau in load progression.

10. Relative Strength & Tiers

Absolute load (kg lifted) is hard to compare across body weights. A 100 kg bench press is impressive at 70 kg body weight but unremarkable at 120 kg. Relative strength normalises performance to body weight:

Relative Strength Ratio
relativeStrength = e1RM / bodyWeight
For dumbbell exercises: e1RM is the per-hand e1RM (bilateral adjustment stripped). This matches the convention used by Symmetric Strength and most standard tables.

11. Composite Strength Score (CSS)

The CSS is a single 0–100 score (graded S to F) that synthesises the quality, trajectory, and output of your training across all three pillars: Level, Momentum, and Process.

Composite Strength Score
CSS = 0.35 × Level + 0.40 × Momentum + 0.25 × Process
All three sub-scores are 0–100. Weights reflect that week-to-week progression direction (Momentum) is the most actionable signal for most athletes, while current strength level provides context and process quality provides diagnostic depth.

Pillar 1: Level (35%)

Level measures how close your current strength is to your all-time personal best, weighted by muscle mass contributed:

Level — PCSA-Weighted Retention
For each exercise:
stdRetention = latestE1RM / peakE1RM
adjRetention = latestAdjE1RM / peakAdjE1RM (fatigue-adjusted)
blendedRetention = 0.5 × stdRetention + 0.5 × adjRetention
activationWeight = Σ(pctMVC × PCSA) for exercise's muscle profile
levelA = Σ(activationWeight × blendedRetention) / Σ(activationWeight) × 100
With body weight data:
levelB = latestRawPSI / peakRawPSI × 100
levelC = avg tierScore (0/33/67/100) across compound lifts
Level = 0.50 × levelA + 0.30 × levelB + 0.20 × levelC
Without body weight: Level = levelA only

Pillar 2: Momentum (40%)

Momentum measures how fast your strength is progressing right now, experience-calibrated so a beginner gaining 2%/week and an elite lifter gaining 0.3%/week can both score 100:

Momentum
ceiling = {Beginner: 3.0, Intermediate: 2.0, Advanced: 1.0, Elite: 0.5} %/wk
slope = 50.0 / ceiling
For each exercise:
pct = 0.5 × pctChangePerWeek + 0.5 × pctChangePerWeekFatigue
score = clamp(50 + pct × slope, 0, 100)
Momentum = weighted avg by session count
0%/wk always maps to 50 (flat momentum). Ceiling%/wk = 100. −ceiling%/wk = 0. The blend of standard + fatigue-adjusted trend prevents always-optimistic readings from fatigue recovery masking as genuine progress.

Pillar 3: Process (25%)

Process measures whether training quality — stimulus, fatigue accumulation, and recovery — is dialled in. Three sub-components:

Process Score
process = 0.40 × INOL_sub + 0.40 × Efficiency_sub + 0.20 × RepDecay_sub
INOL_sub = max(0, 100 − |INOL − optimalCenter| × penaltyRate)
Efficiency_sub = {Q3+: 90, Q1–Q3: 60, <Q1: 25}
RepDecay_sub = {−0.5 to −1.5: 100, −1.5 to −2.5: 70, −0.5 to 0: 65, ≥0: 40, <−2.5: 30}
Sub-scores default to 50 (neutral) when data is insufficient. This prevents punishing new users.

12. Category Efficiency Matrix

The category breakdown (Progress → Category tab) shows how each movement pattern (Horizontal Push, Hip Hinge, etc.) is performing across two dimensions: volume and improvement rate. These are plotted on a 2×2 matrix using the median as the threshold in each dimension:

VolumeImprovement RateQuadrantMeaning
HighHighEfficientOptimal — keep doing what you're doing
HighLowInefficientLots of work, poor return — cut sets, raise intensity
LowHighOpportunityResponding well — more volume here could compound fast

Volume = average weekly tonnage (kg) over 6 weeks. Improvement rate = session-count-weighted average %/week gain across exercises in that pattern.

13. Circuit & Cardio: AMRAP vs EMOM

Circuit Formats

FormatStructureBest For
AMRAP — As Many Rounds As PossibleComplete the full exercise list as many times as possible within the time cap. Rest only when needed. Count rounds + extra reps.General metabolic conditioning, tracking volume capacity over time.
EMOM — Every Minute On the MinuteStart a new exercise (or round) exactly at the top of each minute. Complete the target reps, then rest for the remainder of the minute. The next minute starts automatically.Interval-based conditioning with built-in rest structure; pacing and power output tracking.

Weighted Circuit Logging

Circuits are not limited to bodyweight exercises. Each exercise in a circuit can be assigned an optional weight (kg). When weight is set:

  • The weight is displayed during the active session below the exercise name
  • Every completed result records the weightUsed
  • Post-session summary shows kg·reps tonnage alongside rounds and reps
  • The cardio log entry computes totalVolume = Σ (repsCompleted × weightUsed)
Circuit Tonnage
totalVolume = Σ (repsCompleted_i × weightUsed_i)
Summed across all results in the session. Zero for bodyweight-only circuits.

EMOM Auto-Save

In EMOM sessions, reps are auto-saved when the minute expires — you do not need to tap anything between minutes. The timer and voiceover announcements use a wall-clock derivation that survives app backgrounding and phone locking (background audio mode is enabled).

Session Resume

If the app is killed mid-session, reopening it and navigating to Circuits will automatically restore the session at the correct time position, based on the saved wall-clock start time.

15. The 8 Cross-Domain Cardio Insights

After sufficient data builds (typically 3–5 cardio sessions plus 4+ strength sessions), the app derives 8 second-order insights that cannot be seen from either modality in isolation.

1. HIIT Endurance Trend

Signal: Completed Rounds × Time. Requires ≥ 3 cardio sessions.

OLS slope of completedRounds over sessions. A positive slope (>0.15/session) indicates climbing endurance capacity. A negative slope (<−0.10) indicates decline. Flat otherwise.

2. Fatigue Resistance

Signal: Round Progression × Rep Count. Requires ≥ 2 sessions with ≥ 3 rounds.

For each session, computes the percentage drop from average reps in the first third of rounds to the last third. Mean across last 5 sessions:

Fatigue Resistance
fadePct = (earlyAvgReps − lateAvgReps) / earlyAvgReps × 100
States: Iron Wall (<5%), Normal Fade (5–15%), Early Collapse (>15%)

3. Work Capacity Density

Signal: Reps per Minute × Sessions. Requires ≥ 3 sessions.

Density
density_session = totalReps / durationMinutes
trend_slope = OLS over sessions
States: Accelerating (>+0.3/session), Dropping (<−0.3/session), Holding (otherwise)

4. Circuit Consistency

Signal: Rep Variance × Round Count. Requires ≥ 2 sessions with ≥ 3 rounds.

Standard deviation of total reps per round across each session, averaged. Low variance = consistent effort output. High variance = pacing problems or exercise selection imbalance.

Avg Std DevState
< 2 reps/roundMetronomic — consistent power output every round
2–5 reps/roundVariable — normal variation, some pacing oscillation
> 5 reps/roundErratic — significant early fatigue or pacing failure

5. Cardio-Strength Interference (48-Hour Window)

Signal: HIIT Timing × Strength Feel. Requires ≥ 2 HIIT-adjacent strength sessions.

For each strength session with a feel rating, checks whether a cardio session occurred within the prior 48 hours. Compares feel score (Tired=0, Normal=0.5, Strong=1.0) post-HIIT vs baseline (no prior HIIT).

Interference Effect
diff = postHIITMeanFeel − baselineMeanFeel
Synergistic: diff > +0.12 (cardio improves strength session feel)
Neutral: |diff| ≤ 0.12
Suppressive: diff < −0.12 (cardio impairs strength session feel)
Scientific basis: Concurrent training interference effect (Wilson et al., 2012, J Strength Cond Res meta-analysis): endurance training within 6–24 hours of strength training can impair strength adaptations via AMPK–mTOR pathway competition. The 48-hour window is conservative; most evidence points to 6–24 hours as the highest-interference period.

6. Modality Sequencing

Signal: HIIT Frequency × Strength Scheduling. Requires ≥ 2 of each.

Average gap (days) between cardio session end and next strength session start:

8. Energy System Synergy (24-Hour Window)

Same as Insight 5 but with a tighter 24-hour lookback window. Captures acute metabolic state effects: does a cardio session in the last 24 hours leave you feeling stronger or more drained going into strength work? Long-term divergence between the 24h and 48h windows can indicate whether the interference is acute fatigue (resolves in a day) or cumulative.

16. Readiness Engine

The Readiness score (0–100) appears on the Home screen as a coaching card. It estimates your current physiological preparedness for a productive training session, derived entirely from your workout log without requiring HealthKit.

Readiness Score
base = 68 (neutral)
daysSinceLast ∈ {strength sessions} ∪ {cardio sessions} (unified activity)
daysSinceLast adjustment: 0=+0, 1=+8, 2=+12, 3=+6, 4–6=−4, 7+=−14
frequency (last 7 days): ≥4 sessions=+5, ≤1 session=−8
volume ratio (last 7d vs prior 7d): <0.7=+6 (deload), >1.5=−6 (spike)
score = clamp(base + adjustments, 20, 99)
Volume for strength = tonnage. Volume for cardio = totalReps × 2 (normalising factor). Days since last activity counts both modalities: a 45-minute cardio session counts as a training day for readiness purposes.

Baseline

The baseline is a 30-day personalised average: 65 + min(15, sessions_in_last_30_days). The score is shown relative to this baseline, allowing you to see whether today's readiness is above or below your norm.

Scientific basis: The 1–2 day post-workout readiness peak reflects supercompensation timing (Zatsiorsky & Kraemer, Science and Practice of Strength Training, 2006). The 7+ day penalty reflects detraining onset — strength begins measurably declining within 2–3 weeks of inactivity (Mujika & Padilla, 2000, J Appl Physiol). Volume spike penalty reflects acute overload monitoring (Hulin et al., 2016, BJSM — acute:chronic workload ratio research).

17. Training Streak

The streak counts consecutive calendar days with at least one completed session (strength or cardio). The algorithm uses a set-based approach:

Streak Algorithm
activeDays = Set of calendar days (startOfDay) from both workoutLog and cardioLog
checkDate = startOfDay(today)
if today is not active: checkDate = yesterday
streak = 0; while activeDays.contains(checkDate): streak += 1; checkDate -= 1 day

This means a circuit session on Monday and a strength session on Tuesday both count. A rest day breaks the streak. The app does not penalise you for not training on the day you're currently in (the streak window extends to "today or yesterday" to handle sessions completed earlier the same day).

Why HRV matters: Heart rate variability is the most validated non-invasive proxy for autonomic nervous system recovery status. Multiple meta-analyses (Plews et al., 2012; Buchheit, 2014) have shown HRV-guided training increases performance gains vs fixed periodization. Low HRV = sympathetic dominance = inadequate recovery. High HRV (relative to your baseline) = parasympathetic dominance = ready to train hard.

19. Routines & Templates

Routines (Workout tab → Templates) are weekly training plans. Each routine has a name, a set of exercises assigned to specific weekdays, and an optional list of linked cardio circuits.

Exercise Assignment

When you add an exercise to a routine, you assign it to specific weekdays. On those days, the exercise appears on the Home screen's "Today's Workout" section with last-session performance pre-filled.

Rest Day Planning

Each routine can have rest days marked (Workout tab → Templates → your routine → Rest Days section). A rest day is a weekday integer (1=Sunday … 7=Saturday). When today matches a marked rest day for an active routine, the home screen shows a rest day reminder instead of a workout prompt.

Cardio Circuits in Routines

Circuits can be attached to routines via routine.circuitIds. The Home screen shows any circuit scheduled for today either by its own assignedDays setting or by being linked to a routine that includes today. Both paths are merged (deduplicated by circuit ID) in the todayCircuits computed property.

QR Code Sharing

Routines can be exported as QR codes and imported by scanning. This allows sharing exact exercise selections, sets, and progression targets with training partners.

23. Exercise-Specific Strength Multipliers

Relative strength thresholds are set for barbell reference exercises (bench, deadlift, squat, OHP, row, pull-up). For all other exercises, a multiplier scales these thresholds to reflect how much more or less load that exercise typically produces at the same relative intensity.

Incline Barbell Press0.88×Steeper angle recruits weaker anterior delt more heavily
