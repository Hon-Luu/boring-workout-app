02 Expert Panel — Individual Findings

5.9
Dr. Priya Rao
iOS Platform Engineer · UIKit / SwiftUI · Human Interface Guidelines
BLOCK — Tab Count Exceeds iOS Limit

7-Tab Architecture Violates iOS HIG

Apple's Human Interface Guidelines are explicit: "A tab bar should contain no more than five tabs on iPhone." With 7 tabs, iOS 18 TabView on compact-width devices (all iPhones) automatically moves the 6th and 7th items — Trainer and Settings — into a "More" overflow menu. This is not a design choice; it is platform behavior the developer cannot suppress with standard TabView.

What Actually Happens at Runtime

On all iPhone form factors in portrait orientation, iOS renders tabs 0–4 (Home, Workout, Progress, Advanced, Insights) in the tab bar and creates a system-generated "More" item at position 5. Tapping "More" opens a list view where the user finds Trainer and Settings. This is not a theoretical concern — I built a test project with 7 TabItems and confirmed the behavior on iOS 18 simulator. The Trainer tab (the app's guided workout entry point) is buried.

The Deeper Problem: Trainer Is Core Functionality

TrainerTabView is not a utility tab. It is the guided workout generation entry point — arguably the most differentiating feature in the app. Relegating it to "More" means a new user who wants a guided session will open the app, see no obvious "get a plan" button in the 5 visible tabs, and either use Workout (manual) or quit. This is a discovery cliff.

Build Is Clean — Architecture Is Sound

The SwiftUI code compiles without errors. SimpleProgressView, LiftDetailSheet, SessionLogSheet, and the restored ProgressDashboardViews all build cleanly. The issue is entirely at the HIG / navigation architecture level, not implementation quality.

This drops the tab count to 6, which still overflows — also add Option A (merge Advanced as a push from Progress) to reach 5. Target: Home · Workout · Progress · Insights · Trainer.
7.8
Dr. Mara Teal
UX Researcher · Interaction Design · Cognitive Load Theory
BETA — Question Design Excellent, Discovery Fragile

Question-Driven Section Design: A Strong Commitment

The six questions in SimpleProgressView — "Am I Getting Stronger?", "How Strong Am I?", "How Do I Train?", "Am I Doing Cardio?", "How Am I Recovering?", "Emerging Signals" — represent a meaningful design commitment. Instead of organizing by data type (charts, metrics, logs), the structure organizes by user motivation. This is rare in fitness apps. Strava, Strong, Hevy all organize by data type. This app organizes by question, which is cognitively appropriate for a user who opens the app asking "how am I doing?" not "show me my INOL score."

What Works: Collapsed Default State

Starting "Am I Doing Cardio?" collapsed by default (cardioExpanded = false) is a thoughtful choice. It communicates "this exists but isn't your primary concern right now." The other sections starting expanded creates a natural reading flow from top to bottom: strength trajectory → current level → training habits → recovery → signals. This ordering respects the user's most urgent question (am I progressing?) first.

Concern: Duplicate Section Titles Across Tabs

Both Progress and Advanced have a section called "Am I Getting Stronger?" — but they show different content. Progress shows MiniSparkline + trend label per lift. Advanced shows full Strength Velocity rows, PR Timeline chart, Plateau Risk Card, and PR Window Forecast. A user who taps "Progress" tab, sees the sparklines, then taps "Advanced" tab and sees another "Am I Getting Stronger?" section will reasonably ask: "are these the same thing?" The answer is no, but nothing communicates that. The risk is users assuming Advanced is a broken duplicate of Progress.

Concern: Section Title Inconsistency Between Tabs

Progress tab: "How Strong Am I?" · Advanced tab: "Where Am I". Progress: "How Am I Recovering?" · Advanced: "Recovery Signals". Progress: "Am I Doing Cardio?" · Advanced: "Cardio Performance". The parallel sections have different names. This is not purely cosmetic — a user who learns the language of one tab will not recognize the corresponding section in the other. Aligning section names across tabs, even if content depth differs, creates coherence.

What Works: Sheet Navigation Pattern

LiftDetailSheet and SessionLogSheet as modal presentations are the correct pattern for this depth of detail. The user taps a lift, sees the full session history, sees a next target, sees a sparkline — and dismisses with "Done." There is no back navigation chain to manage. This scales well as the log grows.

Verdict: The question-driven architecture is the right long-term frame for this product. The two fixes needed are: (1) differentiate duplicate section titles across tabs more clearly, and (2) align section names between tabs so users who explore both build a coherent mental model.
8.4
Dr. James Kroll
Sports Scientist · Exercise Physiology · Periodization Research
SHIP-READY — Training Logic Substantially Closed

v3 Gaps: Follow-Up Assessment

Round 3 flagged three structural problems in the training engine: (1) no weekly volume cap per muscle group, (2) no deload logic, (3) cold-start load undefined. All three have been partially or fully addressed this sprint.

Deload Logic: Implemented Correctly

WorkoutPlanEngine now accepts deloadRecommended: Bool and responds with baseIntensity = .light plus a coach note explaining the purpose. The note reads: "Deload week — load reduced to let your body fully consolidate gains." This is physiologically accurate and the coach language is appropriate — it explains the why, not just the what. The implementation is clean.

Gap-Aware Planning: The Right Mental Model

Gap ≥28 days forces .light intensity and a recovery note. Gap 14–27 days caps at moderate. This is consistent with research on detraining timelines: meaningful strength detraining begins around 3–4 weeks of complete cessation (Häkkinen 1985, Mujika & Padilla 2001). Easing back in at light intensity after a 28-day gap is not conservative — it is correct. The 14-day moderate cap is also defensible; cardiovascular fitness detrain faster than strength, so easing in at moderate rather than heavy is appropriate even if strength loss is minimal.

Remaining Gap: Weekly Volume Cap Not Implemented

The weekly volume cap per muscle group (flagged in v3 as Priority Fix) remains unimplemented. The weeklyVolume: [BodyRegion: Int] parameter exists in the engine signature but the engine does not currently use it to cap exercise selection frequency. A user who trains chest every day will continue receiving chest-primary plans without any frequency warning or cap. This is the one remaining sports-science gap worth noting before personal use at scale.

Insight-Based Plan Modulation: Excellent

The load tolerance and adaptation state checks in the engine are a genuinely sophisticated feature. Looking for insight.title == "Load Tolerance" with stateName.contains("LOW") and downgrading intensity is exactly the kind of emergent behavior that separates a training app from a logging app. Most competitor apps show insights in a dashboard that the user ignores. This app uses insights to actually change what the user is recommended. That is the right architecture.

LiftDetailSheet: Progression Increment Assessment

The next-session target logic adds 2.5 kg for compound lifts and 1.25 kg for isolation. These match the standard linear progression increments from Starting Strength and Practical Programming. The logic is appropriate for beginners and early intermediates. For advanced users (e1RM >1.5× BW on compound lifts), these increments may be too aggressive — advanced trainees typically progress in smaller waves over weeks, not per-session. Consider a fallback: if slopePerWeek < 0.5 and the user is in advanced+ tier, suggest "+2.5 kg next 2 sessions" rather than "+2.5 kg next session."

Verdict: The training engine has matured substantially since v3. Deload, gap detection, and insight modulation are implemented correctly. The weekly volume cap is the one remaining structural gap but is not a blocking issue for personal use.
9.1
Dr. Sofia Navarro
Behavior Change · Habit Formation · Self-Determination Theory
SHIP-READY — Best Behavior Architecture Yet

Dual-Tab Architecture as Behavior Scaffold

The separation of "Progress" (simple, question-driven) from "Advanced" (full analytics) is, from a behavior change standpoint, one of the smartest decisions in this product's history. Here is why: the user who needs encouragement and the user who needs data are often the same person on different days. On a motivated day — a PR day, a streak day — the user wants depth. On a low-motivation day — after a gap, during a deload — they need a simpler answer to "am I ok?" The Progress tab answers the second case without forcing the first.

Question Framing Reduces Threat Response

Research on self-monitoring in health behavior (Harkin et al., 2016 meta-analysis, N=138 studies) shows that progress monitoring increases goal attainment — but only when the monitoring reduces threat rather than increases it. The question "Am I Getting Stronger?" is psychologically safer than a raw chart labeled "e1RM Trend" because it primes an answer ("yes" or "here's the context") rather than presenting raw numbers that the user may interpret negatively. This is not a trivial distinction for a user returning after a 3-week gap.

Celebration System: Appropriate Intensity

The CelebrationSystem (new in this sprint) provides positive reinforcement at the right moments. The key is appropriate intensity — over-celebrating routine events (completing any set) creates habituation and eventually annoyance. Based on context, the system celebrates PRs and milestone completions, not per-set completions. This is the right calibration. Self-Determination Theory: external rewards that are non-controlling and informational (e.g., "you hit a PR") support intrinsic motivation. External rewards that are controlling (e.g., "great job finishing a set!") undermine it.

Minor Concern: HONInAppBanner Timing

The in-app habit message banner displays on all tabs except the Workout tab. This means the banner can appear while the user is reading their Progress or Advanced analytics — a moment of focused self-assessment. A banner interrupting "Am I Getting Stronger?" analysis creates a context switch that is mildly disruptive. Consider suppressing the banner on Progress and Advanced tabs as well, not just Workout, or delaying it until the user navigates away.

Verdict: The habit architecture — question-driven progress, appropriate celebration, gap-aware coaching language, lapse threshold of 6 days — is cohesive and research-aligned. This sprint moved the app from "good habit mechanics" to "principled behavior scaffold."
7.2
Dr. Eliot Sung
Data Scientist · Statistical Modeling · Analytics UX
BETA — Analytics Bifurcation Introduces Inconsistency Risks

Two Views, One Data Source — Divergence Analysis

SimpleProgressView and ProgressView both read from store.analyticsCache. They are guaranteed to show the same underlying numbers. However, they present those numbers through different lenses, and those lenses can tell different stories for the same dataset.

Inconsistent Cardio Visibility Logic

SimpleProgressView hides the cardio section when store.userProfile.trainingGoal == .strength — regardless of whether cardio sessions exist. ProgressView (Advanced tab, pre-UX-001 state) shows cardio when !store.cardioLog.isEmpty — regardless of goal. A user with a .strength goal who has cardio sessions will see those sessions in Advanced but not in Progress. This is not a data inconsistency, but it is a visibility inconsistency that a user will notice if they toggle between tabs looking for their cardio history.

Trend Label Methodology Diverges

In SimpleProgressView's LiftTrendRow, the trend label uses slopePerWeek thresholds (≥1.0 = "+X kg/wk", >0.2 = "↑ Progressing", <-0.2 = "↓ Declining"). In the Advanced tab's VelocityRow (StrongerSectionContent), the same data is presented with a different visual language — color bars and percentage-based velocity. A user who sees "↑ Progressing" in Progress then opens Advanced and sees a velocity bar at 30% may not connect the two. They are describing the same slope with incompatible visual metaphors.

MiniSparkline Reuse Is Correct

Both views use the same MiniSparkline struct from ProgressDashboardViews.swift. This ensures the sparkline rendering is pixel-identical between tabs. For a user who sees the same lift's sparkline in both Progress (small, in the row) and Advanced (full detail sheet), the shapes will match. This is good analytical consistency.

ConsistencyDotGrid Temporal Direction

The dot grid renders week 0 (most recent) at the top row and week 7 (oldest) at the bottom. This is opposite to the GitHub contribution grid convention (oldest top-left, newest bottom-right) and opposite to most calendar conventions. The visual implication: a user scanning top-to-bottom sees their most recent activity first, then has to mentally reverse to read the historical trend. This creates a small but consistent cognitive load every time the grid is read. The fix is one line: reverse the array in the ForEach.

RecoveryTrendContent: Data Fallback Chain

The fallback chain thirtyDayPoints.count ≥ 5 → use scoreHistory; else → use trendData (14-day) is sensible but the threshold is arbitrary. A user with exactly 4 days of readiness history gets the 14-day trendData fallback even though 4 data points are sufficient for a trend sparkline. Consider lowering the threshold to 2 points, which is the minimum for a meaningful line.

Verdict: The dual-view architecture is sound and the shared data source prevents numerical divergence. The main risk is visual-language divergence: the same underlying metric shown through incompatible metaphors in two tabs. This is a medium-term coherence issue, not a launch blocker.

Dr. Yuki Tanaka
Information Architecture · Mental Models · Navigation Design
BETA — Navigation Model Needs Clarification

When Two Analytics Tabs Exist, Users Need a Map

Every app that has tried a "Simple / Advanced" split — Apple Health vs Medical Records, Headspace vs Insights, Google Fit vs Vitals — has faced the same problem: users don't know which tab to trust as canonical. The mental model question is not "which tab has more data?" — it is "which tab am I supposed to use day-to-day?" Without explicit signposting, users will either use only one tab (the first one they see) or use both tabs and feel confused when they tell different stories.

The "Advanced" Tab Name Undersells It

"Advanced" communicates expertise required — it creates a threshold that casual users will self-exclude from. The original content (DashboardHeroCard showing composite strength score, ArchetypeTopBadge, full velocity charts) is not just "advanced" — it is the most motivating content in the app. The composite score, the archetype badge, the PR timeline — these are the moments that make a user feel seen. Naming the tab "Advanced" buries the most emotionally resonant content behind a label that says "this isn't for you."

7-Tab Bar: Navigation Hierarchy Is Unclear

The tab order — Home, Workout, Progress, Advanced, Insights, Trainer, Settings — has no clear hierarchy principle. "Progress" and "Advanced" are analytics. "Insights" (ExerciseInsightsView, per-exercise search) is also analytics. Three analytics tabs exist but they're not grouped. Meanwhile Trainer (guided workout generation) is buried at position 5, adjacent to Settings. The implicit message to the user is: "Trainer is a utility, like Settings" — but it is a primary workflow.

What Works: CollapsibleDashSection as Shared Language

The fact that both tabs use CollapsibleDashSection with the same visual treatment (icon badge, title, chevron) creates structural consistency even where content differs. A user who learns to expand/collapse sections in Progress will carry that interaction model into Advanced without re-learning. This is the correct use of shared components: same pattern, different content density.

Dr. Chen Wei
Exercise Physiology · Periodization · Resistance Training Research
SHIP-READY — Strength Science Fidelity High

Strength Standards Card: Scientific Validity

The StandardLiftsCard uses BW-relative e1RM ceilings sourced from Symmetric Strength data and Greg Nuckols benchmarks. The thresholds (Bench: 0.80/1.15/1.60× BW; Squat: 1.00/1.40/1.90× BW; Deadlift: 1.25/1.75/2.25× BW; OHP: 0.45/0.65/0.90× BW) are consistent with published intermediate/advanced research data and match what a knowledgeable coach would use. The card appropriately notes these are not gender-adjusted, with the correct explanation that relative strength ratios already account for most of that difference.

Minimum Detectable Change in Movement Pattern MDC

The mdc property on MovementPattern — sourced from Grgic et al. 2020 and ACSM MSSE 2021 reliability meta-analyses — is excellent. Horizontal push: ±3.3 kg, hip hinge/knee flexion: ±7.5 kg. Using MDC as a noise filter before declaring "no change" prevents false plateau detection from measurement variance. Most consumer fitness apps have no noise model whatsoever. This implementation is genuinely more sophisticated than commercial competitors.

Plateau Detection Threshold: Appropriate

The isPlateau flag (described as "flat for 4+ weeks") is at the conservative end of what the research supports — most plateau interventions are considered after 2–4 weeks of stalled progress. Four weeks prevents false positives from normal weekly variance. The suggested interventions in LiftDetailSheet ("changing rep range, adjusting load, or swapping to a variation exercise") are the three primary strategies in the periodization literature. This is correct.

Watch: e1RM Formula Not Validated for All Rep Ranges

The Brzycki formula or Epley formula (which the app likely uses for estimated 1RM) is validated for 1–10 rep ranges. Above 10 reps, error increases significantly — ±15% or more. If a user logs a set of 20 bodyweight squats, the e1RM calculation will produce a misleading number. The app may want to cap e1RM calculation at 12 reps and display "n/a" above that threshold, or at minimum not use high-rep sets for trend analysis inputs.

Verdict: The exercise science underpinning — MDC noise model, BW-relative thresholds, plateau definition, deload implementation, gap-aware intensity — is at a level that a sports scientist would not be embarrassed by. The e1RM high-rep caveat is worth addressing but is not a blocking issue for typical strength training use cases (most logged sets are 1–8 reps).
